On 1 October 2021, the Maritime Archaeology and Heritage Institute (MAHI) was awarded funding for a 2-year pilot project titled the Inventory of Maritime Archaeology in Pakistan (IMAP) by Arcadia - a charitable fund of Lisbet Rausing and Peter Baldwin. The aim of IMAP is to document the endangered maritime archaeology and heritage of the Sindh coastline and the Indus deltaic region of Southern Pakistan.

This is the first project of its kind in the country, as Maritime Archaeology is not an established discipline in Pakistan. IMAP will use open-source remote sensing data analysis to locate, identify and document maritime archaeological sites along the coast of Sindh. The key deliverable of IMAP will be an open-access database that will serve as a comprehensive inventory of maritime archaeological sites, enable future scientific research and support effective protections for endangered heritage in Pakistan.
“This is an exciting and long-awaited opportunity for Pakistani archaeologists and students of archaeology to get involved in a new branch of the discipline. Through IMAP, we hope to build local capacity for archaeological documentation, historical research, as well as other specialised skills unique to maritime archaeology.”

Heba Hashmi, CEO MAHI and Principal Investigator on IMAP



The Pakistani coastline has played a pivotal role in international maritime trade networks throughout history. This dates back to the Indus Civilisation (3rd millennium BC), which has been linked to the province of Meluhha that had trading links with Mesopotamia, and continues up to the port established on the Indus Delta (4th century BC) by Alexander the Great, to pre-Islamic and Islamic trade in the Western Indian Ocean from the mercantile stronghold of Debal (1BC-13th century AD), to trade between the Mughal Empire and the East India Company, links between the Makran coast and the Omani Sultanate, and extending into the British Colonial period. Evidence in the form of shipwrecks, cargo, or harbor structures in this region is so far unexplored.
“Arcadia funds the documentation of endangered cultural heritage around the world. We are delighted to support the work of the Maritime Archaeology and Heritage Institute to create the first inventory of maritime archaeology for the country. The inventory will enhance the appreciation and protection of Pakistan’s maritime heritage.”

Mike Heyworth, consultant to Cultural Programmes at Arcadia



An initial, desk-based phase of IMAP was generously supported by the Endowment Fund Trust for Preservation of the Heritage of Sindh (EFT), which involved setting up basic research and operational infrastructure, identifying existing historical sources and datasets that can be used to understand the maritime archaeological resource, and preparing an archaeological potential assessment for the Pakistani coastline.
“Maritime archaeological research is a collaborative effort between experts from a range of different disciplines including archaeologists, historians, marine geomorphologists, heritage managers, architects and even museum curators. We are looking forward to many fruitful collaborations with both local and international experts over the coming years to help develop the inventory.”

Amer Bazl Khan, Director MAHI and Principal Investigator on IMAP



About Arcadia: Arcadia is a charitable fund of Lisbet Rausing and Peter Baldwin. It supports charities and scholarly institutions that preserve cultural heritage and the environment. Arcadia also supports projects that promote open access and all of its awards are granted on the condition that any materials produced are made available for free online. Since 2002, Arcadia has awarded more than $910 million to projects around the world. For more information, please visit https://www.arcadiafund.org.uk/
